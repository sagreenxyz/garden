## Course Overview

Motivational Interviewing (MI) is an evidence-based, person-centered counseling style designed to strengthen a person's own motivation and commitment to change. Developed by William R. Miller and Stephen Rollnick, MI has broad applicability across nursing practice settings — from primary care to acute care, mental health, substance use, chronic disease management, and health promotion.

This course equips BSN students with the foundational knowledge, core skills, and clinical judgment needed to integrate MI into everyday nursing interactions. Students will learn to evoke change talk, navigate sustain talk and discord, align MI with the Stages of Change model, uphold ethical obligations, and document encounters appropriately.

---

## Learning Objectives

By the end of this course, students will be able to:

1. **Define** Motivational Interviewing and distinguish it from traditional advice-giving and confrontational approaches. *(Bloom's: Remember)*
2. **Describe** the four processes of MI — Engaging, Focusing, Evoking, and Planning — and identify nurse behaviors associated with each. *(Bloom's: Understand)*
3. **Apply** OARS skills (Open questions, Affirmations, Reflective listening, Summaries) in clinical interactions to elicit and explore patient perspectives. *(Bloom's: Apply)*
4. **Differentiate** change talk from sustain talk and explain how each type of patient language is associated with behavior change outcomes. *(Bloom's: Analyze)*
5. **Recognize** signs of discord in the therapeutic relationship and respond using MI-consistent strategies to restore collaboration. *(Bloom's: Analyze)*
6. **Apply** the Stages of Change (Transtheoretical Model) to individualize MI strategies for patients at varying levels of readiness. *(Bloom's: Apply)*
7. **Evaluate** ethical considerations specific to MI, including autonomy, beneficence, non-maleficence, and justice in behavior change contexts. *(Bloom's: Evaluate)*
8. **Construct** accurate, professional documentation of MI interactions in the clinical health record. *(Bloom's: Create)*

## Module 1: Foundations of Motivational Interviewing

### Overview

This module introduces MI as a collaborative, goal-oriented style of communication designed to strengthen personal motivation for change. Students explore the historical development of MI, the research evidence supporting its effectiveness, and the core spirit that distinguishes MI from directive counseling approaches.

### Topics

- Historical context: Miller & Rollnick's development of MI in addiction counseling
- What MI is and what it is not (common misconceptions)
- The MI spirit: Partnership, Acceptance, Compassion, Evocation (PACE)
- Evidence base: effectiveness across clinical populations and settings
- MI vs. the Righting Reflex — understanding the nurse's impulse to advise and correct

### Key Concepts

- **Motivational Interviewing (MI):** A collaborative conversation style for strengthening a person's own motivation and commitment to change.
- **Righting Reflex:** The natural tendency of clinicians to want to fix problems by telling patients what to do.
- **PACE:** The four elements of MI spirit — Partnership, Acceptance, Compassion, Evocation.
- **Ambivalence:** The normal state of holding simultaneous and conflicting motivations about change.

### Discussion Questions

1. Think of a clinical situation where you naturally wanted to tell a patient what to do. How might an MI-informed approach have changed that interaction?
2. How does the concept of "autonomy support" align with the QSEN competency of Patient-Centered Care?
3. Why is it important for nurses to recognize and manage the righting reflex?

## Module 2: The Four Processes of MI

### Overview

MI is organized into four overlapping and recursive processes: Engaging, Focusing, Evoking, and Planning. This module explores each process in depth, with attention to how nurses move fluidly between them in clinical encounters.

### Topics

- **Engaging:** Establishing a therapeutic relationship built on trust and respect
- **Focusing:** Developing a shared agenda and identifying the direction of the conversation
- **Evoking:** Eliciting the patient's own motivations for change
- **Planning:** Building commitment and developing a change plan with the patient

### Key Concepts

- **Engaging:** The relational foundation — creating a safe, non-judgmental environment
- **Focusing:** Identifying what matters most to the patient and clarifying the purpose of the conversation
- **Evoking:** Drawing out the patient's personal reasons for change (change talk)
- **Planning:** Collaboratively developing a realistic and patient-driven action plan

### Clinical Application

In a 15-minute nursing assessment, the four processes might look like this:

- *Engaging (0–3 min):* Warm greeting, open posture, reflective listening, normalizing the visit
- *Focusing (3–5 min):* "What's most on your mind today?" — identify the priority together
- *Evoking (5–10 min):* "What makes you want to manage your diabetes more effectively?" — explore reasons, values, importance
- *Planning (10–15 min):* "What's one small step you could take this week?" — action planning, confidence-building

### Discussion Questions

1. How does Engaging differ from simply gathering assessment data?
2. Can Evoking happen before Focusing is complete? Why or why not?
3. What happens when a nurse moves to Planning before the patient is ready?

### Readings and Resources

- Miller, W. R., & Rollnick, S. (2023). *Motivational Interviewing* (4th ed.). *(Chapters 4–7)*
- Rollnick, S., Miller, W. R., & Butler, C. C. (2008). *Motivational Interviewing in Health Care.* *(Chapters 3–5)*

## Module 3: OARS — Core Communication Skills

### Overview

OARS is the foundational skillset of MI. These four techniques — Open questions, Affirmations, Reflective listening, and Summaries — help nurses create a collaborative, person-centered conversation that strengthens the patient's own resolve for change.

### Topics

- **Open Questions:** Inviting the patient to share their perspective, experiences, and goals
- **Affirmations:** Recognizing and affirming patient strengths, efforts, and values
- **Reflective Listening:** Reflecting back the patient's meaning — simple, complex, double-sided
- **Summaries:** Periodic synthesis of the conversation to reinforce change talk and demonstrate active listening

### Key Concepts

| Skill | Definition | Example |
|-------|-----------|---------|
| Open Question | A question that invites elaboration, not a yes/no response | "What would be different for you if you started exercising regularly?" |
| Affirmation | A genuine statement that acknowledges the patient's strengths or efforts | "It sounds like you've worked really hard to make this change." |
| Simple Reflection | Mirrors back the patient's words | Patient: "I'm worried about my diet." Nurse: "You're concerned about what you're eating." |
| Complex Reflection | Adds meaning, feeling, or inferred content | "You're not just worried about your diet — it sounds like you're thinking about your future health." |
| Double-Sided Reflection | Reflects both sides of the patient's ambivalence | "On one hand, you enjoy smoking; on the other, you're scared about your lung health." |
| Summary | Collects and reflects back key patient statements | "Let me make sure I understand what you've shared today..." |

### Practice Exercises

**Exercise 3.1 — Open vs. Closed Questions**

Convert the following closed questions to open questions:
1. "Do you take your medications every day?"
2. "Have you thought about quitting smoking?"
3. "Are you exercising?"

**Exercise 3.2 — Identifying Reflections**

Classify each nurse statement as a simple reflection, complex reflection, or double-sided reflection:
1. "You said you're not sure if you're ready."
2. "It seems like part of you wants to make this change, but another part is worried about failing."
3. "You enjoy drinking with friends, but you're also worried about your liver."

**Exercise 3.3 — Writing Affirmations**

For each patient statement, write an affirmation that is genuine, specific, and does not begin with "I":
1. Patient: "I tried to cut back on salt last week, but it was really hard."
2. Patient: "I've been coming to these appointments even though it's hard to get here."

### Discussion Questions

1. Why is it important that affirmations focus on the patient rather than start with "I'm proud of you"?
2. How does a double-sided reflection differ from confrontation or challenging a patient?
3. What is the risk of using too many closed questions in an MI encounter?

### Readings and Resources

- Miller, W. R., & Rollnick, S. (2023). *Motivational Interviewing* (4th ed.). *(Chapters 8–12)*
- Rollnick, S., Miller, W. R., & Butler, C. C. (2008). *Motivational Interviewing in Health Care.* *(Chapters 6–8)*

## Module 4: Change Talk and Sustain Talk

### Overview

The language a patient uses during a clinical conversation is a powerful indicator of their readiness and commitment to change. This module focuses on recognizing and responding to change talk (language that favors change) and sustain talk (language that favors the status quo), and how the nurse's responses can strengthen or weaken the patient's motivation.

### Topics

- Defining and categorizing change talk: DARN-CAT framework
  - **Preparatory change talk:** Desire, Ability, Reasons, Need
  - **Mobilizing change talk:** Commitment, Activation, Taking Steps
- Sustain talk: Definition, examples, and appropriate responses
- The role of the nurse in cultivating change talk and not "arguing for change"
- Using rulers and scaling questions to evoke importance and confidence

### Key Concepts

| Type | Description | Example |
|------|-------------|---------|
| Desire | Wanting to change | "I want to quit smoking." |
| Ability | Believing they can change | "I think I could do it if I tried." |
| Reasons | Arguments for change | "If I don't change, I'll lose my leg." |
| Need | Feeling urgency to change | "I have to do something about my weight." |
| Commitment | Intending/deciding to change | "I will start walking every day." |
| Activation | Being ready, prepared | "I'm ready to make an appointment with the dietitian." |
| Taking Steps | Already acting | "I've been keeping a food journal this week." |
| Sustain Talk | Reasons to stay the same | "I've been smoking for 20 years — it's who I am." |

### Clinical Skill: Importance and Confidence Rulers

> "On a scale of 0 to 10, how important is it to you to [change behavior]?"
> "On a scale of 0 to 10, how confident are you that you could make this change?"

Follow up by asking: "Why did you give it a [number] and not a lower number?" — this evokes change talk and builds self-efficacy.

### Practice Exercises

**Exercise 4.1 — Identifying Change Talk vs. Sustain Talk**

Label each patient statement as change talk (and specify DARN-CAT type) or sustain talk:
1. "I know I should exercise more, but I just don't have the time."
2. "I've already cut out fast food twice this week."
3. "I really want to be around for my grandkids."
4. "I've tried dieting before and it never works."
5. "I think I could probably manage my stress better if I had some help."

**Exercise 4.2 — Responding to Change Talk**

For each change talk statement, write a nurse response that reflects and amplifies it:
1. Patient: "I want to be healthier so I can keep up with my kids."
2. Patient: "I've been cutting back on alcohol — I skipped drinking last weekend."

### Discussion Questions

1. Why should a nurse avoid "arguing for change" even when the patient's health is at serious risk?
2. How can sustain talk be a valuable part of the clinical conversation rather than an obstacle?
3. What does research show about the relationship between change talk frequency and patient outcomes?

### Readings and Resources

- Miller, W. R., & Rollnick, S. (2023). *Motivational Interviewing* (4th ed.). *(Chapters 13–16)*
- Moyers, T. B., & Martin, T. (2006). Antecedents and consequences of therapist ambivalence. *Journal of Substance Abuse Treatment, 30*(4), 271–275.

## Module 5: Discord — Understanding and Resolving Resistance

### Overview

In MI, what was historically called "resistance" is now understood more precisely as **discord** — a signal that the nurse-patient relationship has hit a rough patch, often because the nurse has moved ahead of the patient or has been directive rather than collaborative. This module teaches students to recognize discord, understand its relational origins, and respond in MI-consistent ways.

### Topics

- Reframing resistance as discord: a relational signal, not patient pathology
- Types of discord: sustaining, discord responses (defensiveness, arguing, interrupting, disengaging)
- Nurse behaviors that create discord (confronting, directing, moralizing, warning)
- MI-consistent responses to discord: reflect, shift focus, affirm autonomy, reframe, side with the negative

### Key Concepts

- **Discord:** Interpersonal friction in the therapeutic relationship, often indicating the nurse-patient collaboration has broken down.
- **Autonomy affirmation:** Explicitly acknowledging the patient's right to make their own decisions ("Ultimately, this is your choice").
- **Side with the negative:** A paradoxical technique where the nurse acknowledges the difficulty of change ("Maybe this really isn't the right time for you").
- **Reframing:** Offering a new perspective on the patient's experience without dismissing their concerns.

### Role-Play Scenario 5.1: The Reluctant Patient

> **Patient (52 M, hypertension):** "You keep telling me to cut out salt, but I've been eating the same way my whole life and I feel fine. You don't know what it's like."
>
> **Task:** Using MI-consistent discord responses, write the nurse's next 3 responses. Avoid lecturing, warning, or confronting.

### Discussion Questions

1. How does labeling patient behavior as "resistant" affect the therapeutic relationship and the nurse's own approach?
2. What is the difference between affirming patient autonomy and giving up on health promotion?
3. Describe a clinical situation where a nurse's behavior — not the patient's — created the discord.

### Readings and Resources

- Miller, W. R., & Rollnick, S. (2023). *Motivational Interviewing* (4th ed.). *(Chapter 19)*
- Rollnick, S., Miller, W. R., & Butler, C. C. (2008). *Motivational Interviewing in Health Care.* *(Chapter 9)*

## Module 6: Stages of Change (Transtheoretical Model)

### Overview

The Transtheoretical Model (TTM), developed by Prochaska and DiClemente, describes behavior change as a progression through distinct stages. Aligning MI strategies with the patient's current stage of change enables nurses to meet patients where they are and avoid mismatched interventions.

### Topics

- The five stages of change: Pre-contemplation, Contemplation, Preparation, Action, Maintenance
- Relapse as a normal part of the change process (not failure)
- Matching MI strategies to each stage
- Using TTM assessment tools (readiness rulers, staging questions)
- Decisional balance: exploring pros and cons of change

### Stages and MI Strategies

| Stage | Patient Characteristics | MI Strategy |
|-------|------------------------|-------------|
| Pre-contemplation | Not considering change; unaware or unwilling | Raise awareness without confrontation; plant seeds of doubt; affirm autonomy |
| Contemplation | Aware of the problem; ambivalent about change | Explore ambivalence; develop discrepancy; double-sided reflections |
| Preparation | Intending to change soon; planning | Strengthen commitment; explore options; discuss barriers |
| Action | Actively making change | Affirm change talk; problem-solve obstacles; support self-efficacy |
| Maintenance | Sustaining change over time | Reinforce accomplishments; explore relapse risk; plan coping strategies |
| Relapse | Return to previous behavior | Normalize; re-engage without judgment; revisit motivation |

### Clinical Application: Staging Assessment

Ask: *"Are you currently [target behavior]?"*
- No, and not thinking about it → **Pre-contemplation**
- No, but thinking about it → **Contemplation**
- No, but planning to start soon → **Preparation**
- Yes, but recently started → **Action**
- Yes, for more than 6 months → **Maintenance**

### Practice Exercises

**Exercise 6.1 — Stage Identification**

Identify the stage of change for each patient and describe one MI-consistent nursing response:
1. Patient: "I know smoking is bad, but I'm not ready to quit. I don't really want to."
2. Patient: "I've been thinking about starting to walk, but I keep putting it off."
3. Patient: "I joined a gym last month and I've been going three times a week."
4. Patient: "I quit drinking for 8 months, but I relapsed last week."

### Discussion Questions

1. Why is it unhelpful to assume all patients with unhealthy behaviors are in the Contemplation stage?
2. How should a nurse respond differently to a Pre-contemplation patient versus an Action-stage patient regarding the same health behavior?
3. How does the concept of relapse as a normal part of change affect nursing attitudes and reduce stigma?

### Readings and Resources

- Prochaska, J. O., & Velicer, W. F. (1997). The transtheoretical model of health behavior change. *American Journal of Health Promotion, 12*(1), 38–48.
- Miller, W. R., & Rollnick, S. (2023). *Motivational Interviewing* (4th ed.). *(Chapter 17)*

## Module 7: Ethics in Motivational Interviewing

### Overview

MI's collaborative and evocative approach raises important ethical questions for nurses: When does supporting autonomy conflict with beneficence? Is MI ever manipulative? How do power dynamics in the nurse-patient relationship affect the ethical use of MI? This module examines these questions through nursing ethics frameworks.

### Topics

- The four principles of biomedical ethics: autonomy, beneficence, non-maleficence, justice
- MI and autonomy: genuine respect vs. covert persuasion
- Power dynamics in clinical settings and their effect on patient self-determination
- Scope of practice: when to refer to a licensed counselor or social worker
- Vulnerable populations: applying MI ethically with patients who are coerced, incapacitated, or in crisis
- Cultural humility and MI: avoiding paternalism and respecting diverse values

### Key Ethical Considerations

1. **Autonomy:** MI is founded on respect for patient autonomy — but nurses must ensure they are evoking the patient's values, not subtly imposing their own.
2. **Beneficence:** Helping patients change requires balancing what is clinically best with what the patient chooses.
3. **Non-maleficence:** Using MI inappropriately (e.g., to pressure a patient into a choice) violates the spirit and ethics of MI.
4. **Justice:** MI skills must be applied equitably; nurses should reflect on how systemic inequities affect a patient's readiness and ability to change.

### Case Study: Ethical Dilemma in MI

> A 34-year-old patient with Type 2 diabetes consistently declines dietary counseling, stating that culturally significant foods are non-negotiable. The nurse believes the patient's diet is causing irreparable harm.

**Discussion questions:**
1. How does the nurse balance beneficence with respect for the patient's cultural autonomy?
2. At what point, if any, should the nurse escalate or refer?
3. How would an MI-consistent approach address this situation without imposing the nurse's values?

### Readings and Resources

- Beauchamp, T. L., & Childress, J. F. (2019). *Principles of Biomedical Ethics* (8th ed.). Oxford University Press. *(Chapter 3–4)*
- Miller, W. R., & Rollnick, S. (2023). *Motivational Interviewing* (4th ed.). *(Chapter 23)*
- American Nurses Association. (2015). *Code of Ethics for Nurses with Interpretive Statements.* Silver Spring, MD: ANA.

## Module 8: Documentation of MI Interactions

### Overview

Accurate and professional documentation of MI-informed encounters ensures continuity of care, protects the nurse legally, and communicates the patient's readiness for change to the interdisciplinary team. This module covers documentation frameworks and practical examples for MI encounters.

### Topics

- Why documentation of behavioral health conversations matters
- SOAP note format applied to MI interactions
- DAR (Data, Action, Response) format for nursing notes
- Key elements to document: stage of change, patient statements (change talk), MI strategies used, patient goals set, referrals made
- Avoiding value-laden language in documentation ("non-compliant," "resistant," "unmotivated")

### Documentation Framework

**SOAP Note Structure for MI Encounters:**

- **S (Subjective):** Patient's own words about the health behavior, readiness, importance, and confidence. Direct quotes from the patient documenting change talk are especially valuable.
- **O (Objective):** Observed behaviors, vital signs, clinical data relevant to the behavior (e.g., HbA1c, BMI, cotinine level).
- **A (Assessment):** Stage of change assessment; clinical judgment about the patient's readiness and ambivalence; identification of priorities.
- **P (Plan):** MI strategies used; patient goals collaboratively developed; follow-up plan; referrals.

### Documentation Example

> **S:** Patient states, "I know I need to quit smoking, and I think I could do it — I just don't know how to start." Patient identifies desire and ability as reasons for considering change. No current cessation attempts in the past 6 months.
>
> **O:** Current smoker, 1 PPD × 15 years. Vital signs stable. O2 sat 96% on room air. BP 142/88.
>
> **A:** Patient in Contemplation stage of change. Change talk present: desire ("I know I need to"), ability ("I think I could do it"). Ambivalence noted regarding perceived difficulty of quitting.
>
> **P:** MI engagement provided. Patient expressed interest in pharmacotherapy options. Referral placed to tobacco cessation program. Importance ruler score: 8/10. Confidence ruler score: 5/10. Goal: Patient to call cessation hotline (1-800-QUIT-NOW) within 1 week. Follow-up at next appointment.

### Language to Avoid in Documentation

| Avoid | Use Instead |
|-------|-------------|
| "Patient is non-compliant" | "Patient declined [specific intervention]; stated [patient's reason in quotes]" |
| "Patient is resistant" | "Patient expressed sustain talk regarding [behavior]; MI engagement continued" |
| "Patient is not motivated" | "Patient in Pre-contemplation stage; not currently considering change" |
| "Patient refuses to cooperate" | "Patient did not agree to [plan]; autonomy affirmed; follow-up scheduled" |

### Discussion Questions

1. How does replacing "non-compliant" with objective, MI-informed language change the way other clinicians view the patient?
2. What is the medicolegal importance of documenting patient change talk and the nurse's MI-consistent responses?
3. Why should a nurse avoid documenting a patient as "resistant" or "unmotivated"?

## Module 9: Practicum and Role-Play Scenarios

### Overview

This module provides structured role-play scenarios for students to practice integrating all MI skills — OARS, change talk recognition, discord response, staging, and documentation — in simulated clinical encounters. Faculty may use these scenarios in skills labs, simulation centers, or online recorded practice submissions.

### Scenario 9.1: Smoking Cessation in Primary Care

**Setting:** Community health clinic  
**Patient:** Maria, 47-year-old woman, 1 PPD smoker × 22 years. Presents for routine wellness visit. Vital signs: BP 148/90, SpO2 95%. No previous cessation attempts.

**Faculty directions:** Student plays the nurse. Patient actor holds ambivalence (Contemplation stage). Student must use OARS, elicit change talk, respond to at least one episode of sustain talk, and collaboratively develop a goal.

**Debriefing checklist:**
- [ ] Did the nurse use open questions (at least 3)?
- [ ] Did the nurse reflect patient statements accurately (at least 2 reflections)?
- [ ] Did the nurse provide an affirmation specific to the patient's strengths?
- [ ] Did the nurse identify and respond to at least one change talk statement?
- [ ] Did the nurse respond to sustain talk without arguing or lecturing?
- [ ] Did the nurse offer a collaborative summary?
- [ ] Did the nurse and patient agree on at least one SMART goal?

---

### Scenario 9.2: Medication Adherence in a Chronic Disease Clinic

**Setting:** Cardiology outpatient clinic  
**Patient:** James, 63-year-old man with heart failure, prescribed diuretics and ACE inhibitor. Reports taking medications "when I remember" (approximately 50% adherence). Stage: Pre-contemplation/Contemplation.

**Focus:** Discord recognition and management; autonomy affirmation; decisional balance.

**Student task:** Engage with the patient without lecturing or prescribing. Identify one episode of discord and respond using an MI-consistent strategy. Document the encounter in SOAP format.

---

### Scenario 9.3: Alcohol Use in an Emergency Department Visit

**Setting:** Emergency Department  
**Patient:** Devon, 28-year-old, presenting after alcohol-related injury. AUDIT-C score = 8 (high risk). Reports drinking 6–8 drinks per day on weekends.

**Focus:** Brief MI intervention in a time-limited setting; FRAMES approach (Feedback, Responsibility, Advice, Menu, Empathy, Self-efficacy); change talk elicitation; Preparation/Action-stage strategies.

**Student task:** Conduct a 10-minute brief MI intervention. Identify Devon's stage of change. Write a DAR nursing note for the encounter.

---

### Scenario 9.4: Dietary Change in a Pediatric Parent Encounter

**Setting:** Pediatric primary care  
**Patient:** Parent of 7-year-old with obesity (BMI >95th percentile). Parent is defensive about the child's diet and reluctant to accept referrals.

**Focus:** Discord management; cultural humility; family-centered MI; respecting parental autonomy while addressing child welfare.

**Student task:** Use MI skills to engage the parent non-confrontationally. Identify at least one piece of change talk from the parent. Navigate one episode of discord. Discuss how cultural values affect food choices in this scenario.

## Module 10: Assessment and Program Wrap-Up

### Overview

This final module assesses student mastery of MI concepts and skills through a summative quiz and a reflective clinical assignment. Students also review key course themes and identify areas for continued professional development.

### Summative Quiz Topics

- Definitions and core concepts of MI and PACE spirit
- The four processes of MI
- OARS classification and application
- Change talk vs. sustain talk (DARN-CAT)
- Discord recognition and response
- Stages of change and matched MI strategies
- Ethical principles applied to MI
- Professional documentation language and frameworks

### Reflective Clinical Assignment

**Assignment: MI Self-Reflection Paper (500–700 words)**

After completing a clinical placement or simulation experience, write a reflective paper addressing the following:

1. Describe a specific patient interaction where you used (or could have used) MI skills. What was the context?
2. Which OARS skills did you use? Which were most natural? Which felt challenging?
3. Did you notice any righting reflex impulses in yourself? How did you manage them?
4. What stage of change was the patient in? How did this affect your approach?
5. What would you do differently in a future interaction?
6. How do you plan to continue developing your MI skills in clinical practice?

**Grading criteria:** Specificity of clinical example (25%), accurate application of MI concepts (25%), depth of self-reflection (25%), professional writing (25%).

### Skills Check: Direct Observation or Video Submission

Students complete a 10-minute recorded or observed role-play encounter demonstrating:
- At least 3 open questions
- At least 2 reflections (at least 1 complex)
- At least 1 affirmation
- At least 1 summary
- Recognition and appropriate response to change talk
- Response to sustain talk without confrontation
- Avoidance of the righting reflex throughout

Faculty score using the [Motivational Interviewing Treatment Integrity (MITI) coding system](https://casaa.unm.edu/inst/MITI%204.2.1.pdf) or an adapted rubric.

### Course Wrap-Up

- MI is a lifelong skill; fidelity improves with practice, feedback, and supervision
- Consider joining MINT (Motivational Interviewing Network of Trainers) or pursuing formal MI training
- Key takeaway: The patient's own motivations are the most powerful driver of change — the nurse's role is to draw them out, not supply them
