## Overview

Hemodynamic stability refers to the state in which the cardiovascular system consistently delivers sufficient oxygen and nutrients to meet the metabolic demands of tissues while maintaining adequate perfusion pressure throughout the body. It is not a single measurement but a dynamic equilibrium sustained by the integrated interplay of heart rate, stroke volume, vascular resistance, intravascular volume, and the oxygen-carrying capacity of the blood. When this equilibrium is preserved, organs receive adequate perfusion, cellular homeostasis is maintained, and physiological reserves remain intact.

Hemodynamic instability, by contrast, occurs when this equilibrium is disrupted to a degree that threatens tissue perfusion and organ viability. It is one of the most common and consequential clinical states encountered in acute and critical care nursing. Patients experiencing hemodynamic instability face dramatically elevated risks of acute organ failure, prolonged mechanical ventilation, nosocomial complications, and death. Recognizing the signs of impending hemodynamic compromise — often before overt hypotension or organ failure develops — is therefore a defining clinical judgment competency for the BSN-prepared nurse.

Hemodynamic stability is relevant across virtually every clinical specialty and care setting. It is foundational to the management of sepsis, heart failure, trauma, hemorrhage, anaphylaxis, pulmonary embolism, postoperative care, and any condition that stresses the cardiovascular system. The nurse's role encompasses continuous assessment, timely intervention, accurate hemodynamic monitoring, administration and titration of vasoactive medications, interprofessional communication, and patient and family education — all within a framework of evidence-based practice.

<Callout type="clinical-alert">
  Hemodynamic instability can progress rapidly to life-threatening shock. Tachycardia, narrowing
  pulse pressure, decreased urine output, and altered mental status may signal early
  decompensation even before frank hypotension develops. Early recognition and escalation are
  critical nursing priorities.
</Callout>

## Pathophysiology

### The Determinants of Cardiac Output

Cardiac output (CO) — the volume of blood ejected by the heart per minute — is the central variable of hemodynamic stability. Normal CO in adults ranges from 4 to 8 liters per minute. It is the product of **heart rate (HR)** and **stroke volume (SV)**:

> **CO = HR × SV**

Stroke volume is itself determined by three interrelated physiological variables: **preload**, **afterload**, and **contractility**. Understanding each determinant is essential to recognizing the cause of hemodynamic instability and selecting appropriate therapeutic interventions.

#### Preload

Preload is the degree of myocardial fiber stretch at end-diastole — the volume of blood in the ventricle just before contraction. It is primarily determined by venous return to the heart and is most directly estimated clinically by central venous pressure (CVP) for the right ventricle and pulmonary artery wedge pressure (PAWP) for the left ventricle. The **Frank-Starling law** describes the relationship between preload and stroke volume: within physiological limits, greater fiber stretch at end-diastole produces a more forceful systolic contraction, increasing stroke volume and cardiac output. However, beyond an optimal preload, further volume loading leads to ventricular overdistension and decreasing contractile efficiency — a concept of central importance in the management of heart failure and acute respiratory distress syndrome (ARDS).

Hypovolemia — from hemorrhage, dehydration, third spacing, or excessive diuresis — reduces preload and is the most common cause of hemodynamic compromise in general medical-surgical settings. Conversely, fluid overload or right ventricular failure can pathologically elevate preload, impairing effective forward flow.

#### Afterload

Afterload is the resistance the ventricle must overcome to eject blood into the systemic or pulmonary circulation. For the left ventricle, afterload is most closely approximated by **systemic vascular resistance (SVR)**, which reflects the tone of peripheral arterioles regulated by the autonomic nervous system, circulating vasoactive hormones (including angiotensin II, norepinephrine, and antidiuretic hormone), and local autoregulatory mechanisms. For the right ventricle, afterload corresponds to pulmonary vascular resistance (PVR).

Elevated afterload — as in uncontrolled hypertension, aortic stenosis, or vasoconstriction — increases cardiac work and can reduce stroke volume, particularly in a heart with compromised contractile reserve. Reduced afterload — as in distributive shock, anaphylaxis, or excessive vasodilator therapy — can cause profound hypotension despite a normal or elevated cardiac output.

#### Contractility

Contractility (inotropy) is the intrinsic ability of the myocardium to generate force independent of preload and afterload. It is influenced by sympathetic nervous system activation, calcium availability, myocardial oxygen supply, and the integrity of myocardial cellular architecture. Impaired contractility — as in cardiomyopathy, myocardial infarction, myocarditis, or the direct myocardial depressant effects of sepsis — reduces stroke volume and cardiac output even when preload and afterload are within normal ranges. Positive inotropic agents (e.g., dobutamine, milrinone) can augment contractility pharmacologically when intrinsic reserve is insufficient.

#### Heart Rate

Heart rate modulates cardiac output independent of stroke volume. Compensatory tachycardia is a universal early response to reduced stroke volume, as the body attempts to maintain adequate cardiac output. However, extreme tachycardia shortens diastolic filling time, reduces coronary artery perfusion (which occurs primarily during diastole), and increases myocardial oxygen demand — all of which can worsen hemodynamic status and precipitate ischemia. Bradycardia, conversely, can reduce cardiac output when stroke volume cannot compensate adequately.

### Mean Arterial Pressure and Tissue Perfusion

**Mean arterial pressure (MAP)** is the time-averaged pressure driving blood through the systemic circulation and is the most clinically meaningful blood pressure metric for assessing organ perfusion. MAP is calculated as:

> **MAP = Diastolic BP + 1/3 (Pulse Pressure)**  
> Or approximately: **MAP = (SBP + 2 × DBP) / 3**

A MAP of ≥ 65 mmHg is widely accepted as the minimum threshold for adequate organ perfusion in the critically ill adult. Values below this threshold indicate hemodynamic compromise requiring immediate intervention. MAP is the product of cardiac output and systemic vascular resistance:

> **MAP = CO × SVR**

This relationship clarifies why hemodynamic instability can result from decreases in either CO or SVR, and why interventions targeting different determinants are needed for different shock states.

### Oxygen Delivery and Consumption

Hemodynamic stability ultimately depends not only on adequate blood pressure but on the delivery of sufficient oxygen to meet tissue metabolic needs. **Oxygen delivery (DO₂)** is the product of cardiac output and arterial oxygen content:

> **DO₂ = CO × CaO₂**

Arterial oxygen content (CaO₂) is determined primarily by hemoglobin concentration and arterial oxygen saturation. Thus, anemia and hypoxemia reduce oxygen delivery independent of hemodynamic parameters. **Oxygen consumption (VO₂)** reflects tissue metabolic demand. Normally, cells extract only a fraction of delivered oxygen (roughly 25%), providing a substantial reserve. When DO₂ falls below a critical threshold relative to VO₂ — the **critical DO₂** — cells shift to anaerobic metabolism, producing lactate. Elevated serum lactate is therefore both a marker of hemodynamic compromise and a guide to resuscitation adequacy.

### Mechanisms of Hemodynamic Instability: The Shock States

Hemodynamic instability severe enough to threaten organ perfusion constitutes **shock** — a state of inadequate oxygen delivery relative to metabolic demand. Shock is classically categorized into four pathophysiological types, each with a distinct hemodynamic profile:

| Shock Type | Preload (CVP/PAWP) | Cardiac Output | SVR | Common Causes |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Hypovolemic** | ↓↓ | ↓ | ↑ | Hemorrhage, dehydration, burns, third spacing |
| **Distributive** | ↓ (relative) | ↑ or normal | ↓↓ | Sepsis, anaphylaxis, neurogenic shock, adrenal crisis |
| **Cardiogenic** | ↑ | ↓↓ | ↑ | AMI, cardiomyopathy, severe valvular disease, arrhythmia |
| **Obstructive** | ↑ (RV) | ↓ | ↑ | Pulmonary embolism, cardiac tamponade, tension pneumothorax |

Recognizing the hemodynamic pattern — not just the blood pressure — is essential for selecting the correct therapeutic approach. Treating distributive shock with the same strategy as cardiogenic shock, for example, can cause serious harm.

<Callout type="note">
  Shock is not synonymous with hypotension. Tissue hypoperfusion can occur with a "normal"
  blood pressure, particularly in patients with baseline hypertension whose MAP is still
  insufficient for their individual autoregulatory threshold. Clinical assessment must go beyond
  blood pressure numbers to include perfusion indicators such as mentation, skin color and
  temperature, capillary refill, and urine output.
</Callout>

## Clinical Presentation / Signs and Symptoms

### Signs of Hemodynamic Stability

A hemodynamically stable patient demonstrates consistent tissue perfusion across organ systems. Expected findings include:

- Blood pressure within the patient's normal or established therapeutic range; MAP ≥ 65 mmHg
- Heart rate 60–100 beats/min with regular rhythm
- Respiratory rate 12–20 breaths/min without labored effort
- Warm, dry, and well-perfused skin with capillary refill ≤ 2 seconds
- Alert and oriented mentation appropriate to baseline
- Urine output ≥ 0.5 mL/kg/hr
- Oxygen saturation ≥ 94% on room air or prescribed supplemental oxygen
- Serum lactate within normal limits (< 2 mmol/L)

### Signs of Hemodynamic Instability

Hemodynamic instability presents across a spectrum from subtle early compensation to overt cardiovascular collapse. Nurses must recognize both early and late signs to intervene before irreversible organ injury occurs.

**Early (Compensated) Signs:**
- Resting tachycardia (HR > 100 beats/min) — often the earliest vital sign abnormality
- Mild anxiety, restlessness, or subtle change in mental status
- Cool extremities or mottled skin distal to the core despite a maintained blood pressure
- Slightly decreased urine output (0.3–0.5 mL/kg/hr)
- Narrowing pulse pressure (SBP − DBP < 30 mmHg) — an important early sign of reduced stroke volume
- Orthostatic hypotension (systolic drop ≥ 20 mmHg or diastolic drop ≥ 10 mmHg when moving from supine to standing)

**Late (Decompensated) Signs:**
- Hypotension (SBP < 90 mmHg or MAP < 65 mmHg, or a decrease of ≥ 40 mmHg from baseline)
- Marked tachycardia with weak, thready peripheral pulses
- Altered mental status, confusion, agitation, or decreased level of consciousness
- Oliguria (< 0.5 mL/kg/hr) or anuria
- Pronounced respiratory distress, tachypnea ≥ 22 breaths/min
- Cool, pale, clammy, or cyanotic extremities
- Prolonged capillary refill > 3 seconds
- Elevated serum lactate > 2 mmol/L

<Callout type="warning">
  Narrowing pulse pressure is an underappreciated early warning sign of hemodynamic compromise.
  A progressive narrowing of the pulse pressure — even with a "normal" systolic blood pressure —
  signals reduced stroke volume and early cardiovascular decompensation. Report this finding
  promptly and assess for additional perfusion indicators.
</Callout>

## Diagnostic Studies and Hemodynamic Monitoring

Accurate diagnosis of the type and severity of hemodynamic compromise, and ongoing monitoring during treatment, requires an integrated approach combining physical assessment with point-of-care testing, non-invasive monitoring, and — in critically ill patients — invasive hemodynamic measurement.

### Non-Invasive Monitoring

**Vital signs** remain the cornerstone of hemodynamic assessment and should be obtained frequently in any patient at risk for instability. Blood pressure, heart rate, respiratory rate, temperature, and oxygen saturation (SpO₂) provide a continuous hemodynamic overview. Automated non-invasive blood pressure (NIBP) cuffs cycle at programmable intervals; however, they may underestimate blood pressure during hypotension and fail to capture beat-to-beat variation.

**Pulse oximetry (SpO₂)** provides continuous estimation of arterial oxygen saturation. It is less reliable during hemodynamic instability because peripheral vasoconstriction reduces the photoplethysmographic signal quality. A poor waveform (low pleth variability index) is itself a sign of peripheral hypoperfusion.

**Electrocardiographic monitoring** is essential in patients with hemodynamic instability to detect arrhythmias — a frequent precipitant or consequence of cardiovascular compromise — as well as ST-segment changes indicating acute myocardial ischemia.

**Echocardiography** — particularly point-of-care ultrasound (POCUS) — has become an invaluable non-invasive tool for rapidly assessing ventricular function, estimating volume status (inferior vena cava collapsibility), identifying pericardial effusion/tamponade, and differentiating cardiogenic from distributive shock at the bedside.

### Laboratory Studies

| Study | Clinical Relevance |
|---|---|
| **Serum lactate** | Marker of anaerobic metabolism; guides resuscitation adequacy; target < 2 mmol/L |
| **Complete blood count (CBC)** | Hemoglobin/hematocrit for oxygen-carrying capacity; WBC for infection |
| **Comprehensive metabolic panel** | Renal function (BUN, creatinine), hepatic function, electrolytes, glucose |
| **Arterial blood gas (ABG)** | pH, PaO₂, PaCO₂, HCO₃⁻ — assess oxygenation, ventilation, and metabolic status |
| **Troponin (I or T)** | Myocardial injury marker; elevated in ACS, PE, and sepsis-induced cardiomyopathy |
| **B-type natriuretic peptide (BNP/NT-proBNP)** | Elevated in volume overload and heart failure; distinguishes cardiac from non-cardiac dyspnea |
| **Coagulation studies (PT, aPTT, INR)** | Detect coagulopathy associated with sepsis, hepatic dysfunction, or DIC |
| **Blood cultures × 2** | Identify infectious etiology before antibiotic initiation when sepsis is suspected |
| **Urinalysis with culture** | Common infection source identification |
| **Point-of-care glucose** | Hypoglycemia or hyperglycemia may precipitate or accompany hemodynamic instability |
| **Serum albumin and total protein** | Oncotic pressure determinants; low albumin facilitates third spacing |

### Invasive Hemodynamic Monitoring

Critically ill patients with hemodynamic instability who require vasoactive medications, complex fluid management, or close titration of cardiopulmonary interventions typically require invasive hemodynamic monitoring.

**Arterial line (A-line):** An indwelling catheter placed in a peripheral artery (most commonly the radial artery) connected to a pressure transducer provides continuous, real-time blood pressure monitoring. The arterial waveform morphology offers additional clinical information: a pulsus paradoxus (inspiratory drop in SBP > 10 mmHg) suggests cardiac tamponade or significant respiratory variation in intrathoracic pressure. Systolic pressure variation (SPV) and pulse pressure variation (PPV) — derived from the A-line waveform during mechanical ventilation — are validated dynamic predictors of fluid responsiveness: a PPV > 12–13% predicts that the patient's stroke volume will increase with fluid administration.

**Central venous catheter (CVC) / Central venous pressure (CVP):** A central venous catheter placed in the superior vena cava allows measurement of CVP (also called right atrial pressure), which is a rough indicator of right ventricular preload and venous return. Normal CVP is 2–8 mmHg. While CVP alone is a poor standalone predictor of fluid responsiveness, trend monitoring and integration with other hemodynamic parameters informs clinical decision-making. CVP also enables infusion of vasopressors, hypertonic solutions, and total parenteral nutrition.

**Pulmonary artery catheter (PAC) / Swan-Ganz catheter:** The PAC enables measurement of right atrial pressure (CVP), right ventricular pressure, pulmonary artery pressure (PAP), pulmonary artery wedge pressure (PAWP — an indirect estimate of left ventricular end-diastolic pressure and preload), cardiac output (via thermodilution), and mixed venous oxygen saturation (SvO₂). These parameters allow comprehensive characterization of the type of shock and real-time assessment of treatment response. While PAC use has decreased with the advent of POCUS and less-invasive alternatives, it remains the gold standard for complex hemodynamic assessment in selected patients.

| Parameter | Normal Range | Clinical Significance |
|---|---|---|
| **MAP** | 70–100 mmHg | Minimum ≥ 65 mmHg for organ perfusion |
| **CVP** | 2–8 mmHg | Estimate of right ventricular preload |
| **PAWP** | 6–12 mmHg | Estimate of left ventricular preload; > 18 mmHg suggests cardiogenic pulmonary edema |
| **Cardiac Output (CO)** | 4–8 L/min | Total flow; must be interpreted with body size |
| **Cardiac Index (CI)** | 2.5–4.0 L/min/m² | CO indexed to body surface area; < 2.2 suggests cardiogenic shock |
| **SVR** | 800–1200 dynes/sec/cm⁵ | Vascular resistance; ↑ in hypovolemic/cardiogenic; ↓ in distributive shock |
| **SvO₂** | 60–80% | Tissue oxygen extraction; < 60% suggests increased extraction due to low CO or DO₂ |
| **ScvO₂** | 70–80% | Central venous surrogate for SvO₂; target ≥ 70% in early goal-directed therapy |
| **Serum lactate** | < 2 mmol/L | Marker of anaerobic metabolism and inadequate tissue perfusion |

<Callout type="note">
  The trend over time — not a single static value — is the most clinically useful aspect of
  hemodynamic monitoring data. A CVP rising from 4 to 12 mmHg over two hours while the patient
  remains hypotensive tells a fundamentally different story from a static CVP of 8 mmHg in a
  stable patient. Interpret all parameters in the context of the full clinical picture and
  trajectory of change.
</Callout>

## Nursing Assessment

### Initial Survey and Primary Assessment

Hemodynamic assessment begins with a rapid, systematic survey following the ABCDE framework (Airway, Breathing, Circulation, Disability, Exposure), with particular attention to circulation:

- **Airway:** Assess patency; a compromised airway can precipitate or worsen hemodynamic instability through hypoxemia.
- **Breathing:** Rate, depth, effort, and oxygen saturation; tachypnea may signal metabolic acidosis (compensatory) or pulmonary edema.
- **Circulation:** Blood pressure (bilateral comparison), heart rate and rhythm, pulse quality (bounding vs. thready), skin color, temperature, moisture, and capillary refill time.
- **Disability:** Level of consciousness (AVPU scale or GCS), orientation, and pupillary response.
- **Exposure:** Review for bleeding sites, rashes (petechiae, urticaria), wounds, indwelling devices, and environmental factors that may affect hemodynamic status.

### Focused Cardiovascular Assessment

Following the primary survey, a focused cardiovascular assessment includes:

**Heart:** Auscultate for rate, rhythm, S1 and S2, additional heart sounds (S3 — volume overload; S4 — decreased compliance), and murmurs indicating valvular dysfunction.

**Peripheral vasculature:** Palpate peripheral pulses bilaterally at multiple sites (radial, brachial, femoral, popliteal, dorsalis pedis, posterior tibial). Rate pulse amplitude on a 0–4 scale. Assess capillary refill, skin turgor, and temperature gradient (central vs. peripheral temperature gap > 7°C indicates peripheral vasoconstriction and reduced perfusion).

**Jugular venous distension (JVD):** With the patient at 45 degrees, assess for visible jugular venous pulsations above the clavicle. JVD at 45 degrees suggests elevated central venous pressure (> 8–10 cm H₂O) and may indicate right ventricular failure, cardiac tamponade, or volume overload.

**Pulmonary:** Auscultate all lung fields for crackles (bibasilar rales suggest pulmonary edema from left ventricular failure), rhonchi (secretions), or absent breath sounds (pneumothorax). Assess respiratory effort and use of accessory muscles.

**Abdomen:** Assess for hepatomegaly, ascites (shifting dullness), abdominal tenderness, and bowel sounds; these findings may indicate right heart failure, abdominal compartment syndrome, or hepatic congestion.

**Renal:** Hourly urine output measurement via indwelling urinary catheter is the most practical bedside marker of renal perfusion. A sustained output < 0.5 mL/kg/hr is an early and sensitive indicator of hemodynamic compromise.

**Extremities:** Assess for bilateral pitting edema, which indicates systemic fluid retention; unilateral edema or erythema raises concern for deep vein thrombosis.

### Ongoing Monitoring

Patients with hemodynamic instability require continuous monitoring parameters reassessed at regular, protocol-defined intervals. The frequency of vital sign measurement, hemodynamic parameter recording, laboratory sampling, and reassessment following interventions should be guided by acuity, clinical trajectory, and unit-specific protocols. Documentation must accurately reflect timing, values, interventions, and patient response to support interprofessional communication and clinical decision-making.

<Callout type="tip">
  The "perfusion triad" — mentation, urine output, and skin perfusion — provides a rapid,
  equipment-free assessment of end-organ perfusion that should be incorporated into every
  patient encounter. Deterioration in any of these three indicators warrants immediate
  reassessment of hemodynamic status, regardless of the blood pressure reading.
</Callout>

## Nursing Interventions

### Establishing Intravenous Access and Volume Management

In most hemodynamically unstable patients, the first priority is establishing adequate intravenous access. Large-bore peripheral intravenous catheters (≥ 18 gauge, preferably two sites) allow rapid volume delivery. When large-volume resuscitation or vasoactive infusions are anticipated, central venous access should be established promptly.

**Fluid resuscitation** is the cornerstone of treatment for hypovolemic, distributive, and some forms of obstructive shock. Key evidence-based principles include:

- **Isotonic crystalloids** (0.9% normal saline or Lactated Ringer's solution) are the first-line resuscitation fluids in most clinical contexts. Balanced crystalloids (Lactated Ringer's, Plasma-Lyte) are preferred over normal saline in large-volume resuscitation to reduce the risk of hyperchloremic metabolic acidosis.
- The **Surviving Sepsis Campaign** recommends a minimum of 30 mL/kg IV crystalloid within the first 3 hours for sepsis-induced hypoperfusion, followed by reassessment of fluid responsiveness before additional fluid administration.
- **Fluid responsiveness** — the likelihood that a bolus will increase stroke volume by ≥ 10–15% — should be assessed before each fluid challenge using dynamic indices (pulse pressure variation, passive leg raise with CO measurement) rather than static parameters (CVP alone).
- **Colloids** (albumin, fresh frozen plasma, packed red blood cells) are used selectively: albumin in selected patients with sepsis, blood products when hemorrhage or severe anemia drives instability.
- **Restrict** fluid administration in patients with cardiogenic pulmonary edema, severe ARDS, or evidence of volume overload; aggressive fluid resuscitation in these populations worsens outcomes.

### Positioning

Patient positioning significantly affects hemodynamic status and should be optimized for each clinical situation:

- **Trendelenburg position** (supine with feet elevated 10–30°) was historically used for hypotension but is no longer recommended, as it does not reliably or sustainably increase cardiac output and may worsen respiratory mechanics. The **passive leg raise (PLR)** maneuver — raising both legs to 45° for 60–90 seconds while measuring CO or pulse pressure — is now preferred as both a diagnostic test of fluid responsiveness and a temporary hemodynamic measure.
- **Semi-recumbent positioning** (30–45° head of bed elevation) reduces aspiration risk, supports respiratory mechanics, and is preferred for intubated and mechanically ventilated patients unless hemodynamic contraindications exist.
- Minimize position changes in hemodynamically unstable patients who have not been stabilized; any repositioning should be performed with continuous vital sign monitoring.

### Airway Management and Oxygenation Support

Hemodynamic instability often accompanies or precipitates hypoxemia, which in turn worsens tissue oxygen delivery. Nursing interventions to support oxygenation include:

- Apply supplemental oxygen via nasal cannula, simple face mask, non-rebreather mask, or high-flow nasal cannula (HFNC) to maintain SpO₂ ≥ 94% (or per provider-specified targets).
- Prepare for potential intubation and mechanical ventilation in patients with progressive respiratory failure or hemodynamic collapse; assemble equipment and assist with intubation per protocol.
- Monitor ABG results to assess oxygenation (PaO₂), ventilation (PaCO₂), and acid-base status as guides to therapy.

### Hemodynamic Monitoring and Documentation

- Ensure pressure transducers are zeroed to the phlebostatic axis (fourth intercostal space, midaxillary line) before recording hemodynamic values; re-zero after patient repositioning.
- Assess arterial waveform morphology continuously, troubleshoot damped waveforms (clots, air bubbles, loose connections), and recognize artifacts.
- Maintain patency of hemodynamic lines using continuous heparinized flush or per institution policy.
- Document all hemodynamic parameters — including timing of values, patient position, and interventions — accurately and at the frequency specified by acuity and protocol.

### Medication Administration and Vasoactive Infusion Management

- Administer vasoactive medications (vasopressors, inotropes, vasodilators) via a dedicated central line lumen using a calibrated infusion pump; never administer through a peripheral IV unless specifically approved by the provider and institution policy, and only as a temporary bridge.
- Titrate vasoactive infusions to specific hemodynamic goals (MAP, CI, SVR, urine output) per provider orders, following the principle of minimum effective dose.
- Monitor for medication-specific adverse effects continuously (see Pharmacological Considerations below).
- Avoid abrupt discontinuation of vasopressor infusions; wean gradually as hemodynamic stability is achieved and reassess continuously.

### Interprofessional Communication and Escalation

The nurse is the primary coordinator of hemodynamic monitoring information in the acute and critical care setting. Use structured communication tools — **SBAR (Situation, Background, Assessment, Recommendation)** — when reporting hemodynamic changes to the provider. Identify escalation triggers in advance (e.g., MAP < 60, lactate > 4, heart rate > 130, urine output < 0.3 mL/kg/hr) and act on them promptly without delay for confirmatory reassurance.

<Callout type="clinical-alert">
  Never delay vasopressor initiation in a patient with confirmed septic shock (MAP &lt; 65 mmHg
  unresponsive to fluid resuscitation) while waiting for a central line. Norepinephrine can be
  safely infused through a large-bore peripheral IV for up to 1 hour as a temporary bridge
  during central line placement. Document clinical rationale clearly.
</Callout>

## Pharmacological Considerations

Pharmacological management of hemodynamic instability targets the specific derangement in the hemodynamic profile. The major drug categories are vasopressors (to increase SVR or CO), inotropes (to increase contractility), vasodilators (to reduce afterload or preload), and antiarrhythmics. The nurse is responsible for accurate preparation, administration, titration, monitoring, and documentation of all vasoactive agents.

### Vasopressors

Vasopressors are agents that increase systemic vascular resistance, thereby raising blood pressure primarily by arterial vasoconstriction. They are indicated when hypotension persists after adequate volume resuscitation or when the hemodynamic profile indicates that reduced SVR is the primary driver of instability (distributive shock).

**Norepinephrine (Levophed)**
- **Class:** Catecholamine; α₁ >> β₁ agonist
- **Mechanism:** Potent arterial vasoconstriction (↑ SVR) with modest positive inotropy; minimal β₂ effect, so little tachycardia
- **Indication:** First-line vasopressor for septic shock; also used in other distributive and vasodilatory states
- **Dose:** 0.01–3 mcg/kg/min IV infusion, titrated to MAP goal
- **Nursing implications:** Monitor MAP continuously; assess for digital ischemia and skin necrosis at extravasation sites; monitor for reflex bradycardia; requires central line for prolonged infusion

**Vasopressin (Antidiuretic hormone analogue)**
- **Class:** Non-adrenergic vasopressor; antidiuretic hormone analogue
- **Mechanism:** V1 receptor-mediated vasoconstriction; also has antidiuretic and procoagulant effects; vasopressor effect is independent of adrenergic receptor upregulation/desensitization
- **Indication:** Adjunct to norepinephrine in refractory septic shock, typically added at fixed low dose to "spare" norepinephrine requirements
- **Dose:** Fixed at 0.03–0.04 units/min IV infusion (not titrated)
- **Nursing implications:** Monitor for hyponatremia, coronary artery spasm, splanchnic vasoconstriction; do not abruptly discontinue

**Phenylephrine (Neo-Synephrine)**
- **Class:** Selective α₁ agonist
- **Mechanism:** Pure vasoconstriction with no direct cardiac effect; may cause reflex bradycardia
- **Indication:** Vasodilatory shock when tachycardia is a concern (e.g., atrial fibrillation with rapid ventricular response); may be used in septic shock when norepinephrine is not available
- **Nursing implications:** Reflex bradycardia may reduce CO in patients without compensatory reserve; avoid in cardiogenic shock

**Epinephrine**
- **Class:** Catecholamine; potent α₁, β₁, β₂ agonist
- **Mechanism:** Increases HR, contractility, and SVR; also causes bronchodilation
- **Indication:** Anaphylactic shock (drug of choice, given IM or IV); refractory septic shock; cardiac arrest
- **Nursing implications:** Marked tachycardia and increased myocardial oxygen demand; risk of lactic acidosis at high doses; monitor glucose (hyperglycemia); requires central line for infusion

### Inotropes

Inotropes increase myocardial contractility and are indicated when low cardiac output is the primary hemodynamic derangement — most commonly in cardiogenic shock or decompensated heart failure.

**Dobutamine**
- **Class:** Synthetic catecholamine; β₁ >> β₂ agonist
- **Mechanism:** Positive inotrope and mild vasodilator (↑ CO, mild ↓ SVR); increases HR; does not significantly raise blood pressure in the absence of adequate preload
- **Indication:** Cardiogenic shock; low CI (< 2.2) with adequate MAP; adjunct in septic shock with cardiomyopathy
- **Dose:** 2.5–20 mcg/kg/min IV infusion
- **Nursing implications:** Monitor HR (tachycardia worsens myocardial oxygen demand); watch for hypotension from vasodilation; tolerance may develop within 72 hours; do not use as primary treatment in hypotension without adequate fluid resuscitation

**Milrinone (Primacor)**
- **Class:** Phosphodiesterase-3 (PDE-3) inhibitor
- **Mechanism:** Positive inotrope and vasodilator; acts independently of β-adrenergic receptors (effective even in patients on chronic β-blocker therapy)
- **Indication:** Cardiogenic shock; acute decompensated heart failure; right ventricular failure (reduces PVR)
- **Dose:** 0.375–0.75 mcg/kg/min IV infusion (loading dose optional; often omitted in hypotension)
- **Nursing implications:** Prolonged half-life (2–3 hours) with renal elimination — dose reduce in AKI; monitor for hypotension; narrow therapeutic window

**Digoxin**
- **Class:** Cardiac glycoside; inotrope and rate-controlling agent
- **Mechanism:** Inhibits Na⁺/K⁺-ATPase, increasing intracellular calcium; positive inotropy; negative chronotropy (AV node slowing)
- **Indication:** Rate control in atrial fibrillation with decompensated heart failure; rarely used for acute hemodynamic support
- **Nursing implications:** Narrow therapeutic index; monitor digoxin level (0.5–0.9 ng/mL for heart failure); hypokalemia, hypomagnesemia, and renal insufficiency increase toxicity risk; monitor for nausea, visual disturbances (yellow-green halos), bradycardia, and dysrhythmias

### Vasodilators

Vasodilators reduce preload, afterload, or both, and are used when elevated SVR is worsening cardiac work or when congestion (elevated preload) is the dominant hemodynamic problem — most commonly in hypertensive urgency/emergency, cardiogenic pulmonary edema, or systemic hypertension.

**Nitroglycerin (Nitrostat, Tridil)**
- **Mechanism:** Predominantly venodilator (↓ preload); at higher doses, also arterial dilation (↓ afterload); coronary vasodilation
- **Indication:** Acute decompensated heart failure with pulmonary edema; angina; hypertensive emergency with cardiac involvement
- **Nursing implications:** Headache (most common); rapidly titratable IV infusion; hypotension risk especially with concurrent phosphodiesterase-5 inhibitors (sildenafil — **contraindicated together**); use non-PVC tubing (nitroglycerin adsorbs to PVC); tolerance develops within 24 hours

**Sodium Nitroprusside (Nipride)**
- **Mechanism:** Arterial and venous dilator (↓ afterload and preload); extremely potent and rapidly acting
- **Indication:** Hypertensive emergency; severe afterload reduction in cardiogenic shock with adequate BP
- **Nursing implications:** Cyanide/thiocyanate toxicity with prolonged use (> 72 hours) or in renal/hepatic failure; protect solution from light (wrap tubing in foil); monitor for signs of toxicity (metabolic acidosis, altered mentation); requires continuous arterial line monitoring

**Hydralazine**
- **Mechanism:** Selective arteriolar dilator (↓ afterload); does not significantly reduce preload
- **Indication:** Hypertension in pregnancy (drug of choice for severe hypertension in pregnancy/eclampsia); IV bolus option in acute hypertension
- **Nursing implications:** Reflex tachycardia; monitor blood pressure every 5–15 minutes after IV administration; peak effect 10–20 minutes after IV dose; lupus-like syndrome with chronic oral use

**Nicardipine (Cardene)**
- **Mechanism:** Dihydropyridine calcium channel blocker; selective cerebral and systemic arterial dilation (↓ afterload)
- **Indication:** Hypertensive emergency, particularly hypertensive encephalopathy and peri-operative hypertension
- **Nursing implications:** Less reflex tachycardia than nitroprusside; predictable, titratable response; peripheral IV compatible

### Antiarrhythmics and Rate-Control Agents

Arrhythmias are both a cause and a consequence of hemodynamic instability. Rate control and rhythm restoration are important adjuncts to hemodynamic management.

- **Amiodarone:** Broad-spectrum antiarrhythmic used for hemodynamically significant ventricular and atrial arrhythmias; administered IV in the acute setting; monitor for QT prolongation, hypotension (with IV loading), bradycardia, and phlebitis
- **Adenosine:** First-line treatment for stable supraventricular tachycardia (SVT); rapidly terminates reentrant arrhythmias through transient AV node blockade; very short half-life (< 10 seconds); administer as rapid IV push followed immediately by a saline flush
- **Metoprolol tartrate (Lopressor):** β₁-selective blocker for rate control in atrial fibrillation/flutter, SVT, and hypertensive urgency; avoid in decompensated heart failure, bradycardia, or bronchospasm
- **Atropine:** Anticholinergic agent for symptomatic bradycardia; first-line treatment in hemodynamically significant sinus bradycardia or AV block pending transcutaneous pacing

<Callout type="clinical-alert">
  All vasoactive medications should be prepared and administered using standardized concentration
  protocols per institutional policy to reduce the risk of dose calculation errors. Double-check
  all vasoactive infusion rates independently with a second nurse before initiating or changing
  the rate. Titration changes must be documented in real time with associated hemodynamic values
  and clinical rationale.
</Callout>

## Patient Education

Patient and family education regarding hemodynamic health must be individualized, culturally sensitive, and provided at an appropriate literacy level. Key areas include:

### Understanding Blood Pressure and Heart Function

Patients benefit from a foundational understanding of how blood pressure and heart function relate to their overall health. Explain that blood pressure reflects the force of blood against artery walls, and that both values in the blood pressure reading (systolic and diastolic) are important. Use plain-language analogies (e.g., "your heart is a pump; blood pressure tells us how hard the pump is working") to make abstract concepts accessible.

### Home Blood Pressure Monitoring

Instruct patients with hypertension, heart failure, or a history of hemodynamic instability on accurate home blood pressure measurement technique:

- Rest quietly for 5 minutes before measuring
- Sit with feet flat on the floor, back supported, arm at heart level
- Use a validated automated cuff sized correctly for the arm
- Measure at the same time each day (morning, before medications)
- Record readings in a log and bring to every appointment
- Know their individual target blood pressure range and when to call the provider

### Medication Adherence

Emphasize that antihypertensive, heart failure, and antiarrhythmic medications must be taken consistently as prescribed to maintain hemodynamic stability. Abrupt discontinuation of beta-blockers can cause rebound hypertension and tachycardia; discontinuation of diuretics can cause rapid fluid accumulation. Educate patients to never stop medications without discussing with their provider, and to contact the healthcare team if side effects interfere with adherence.

### Activity and Lifestyle Modifications

Provide education on evidence-based lifestyle modifications that support hemodynamic health:

- **Dietary sodium restriction** (typically < 2 g/day for heart failure; < 2.3 g/day for hypertension) to reduce fluid retention and vascular resistance
- **Fluid restriction** per individualized provider guidance for heart failure patients
- **Regular aerobic physical activity** as tolerated and per provider guidance — cardiac rehabilitation is recommended for post-MI and heart failure patients
- **Weight management** — even modest weight loss (5–10% of body weight) significantly reduces blood pressure and cardiac workload
- **Smoking cessation** — nicotine causes acute vasoconstriction and long-term endothelial damage
- **Alcohol moderation** — excessive alcohol raises blood pressure and depresses myocardial contractility
- **Stress management techniques** — mindfulness, relaxation exercises, and adequate sleep support sympathetic-parasympathetic balance

### Warning Signs Requiring Emergent Care

Instruct patients and families to call 911 or seek immediate emergency care for:

- Sudden severe headache ("worst headache of my life")
- Chest pain, pressure, or tightness at rest
- Sudden shortness of breath, especially when lying flat (orthopnea)
- Syncope or near-syncope (fainting or feeling faint)
- Sudden confusion, speech difficulty, facial droop, or limb weakness (stroke symptoms — call 911 immediately)
- Palpitations associated with dizziness or near-syncope
- Significant swelling of both legs that is new or worsening rapidly
- Home blood pressure reading consistently > 180/120 mmHg

<Callout type="tip">
  Use the teach-back method to confirm patient and family understanding of hemodynamic warning
  signs. Ask the patient to describe in their own words what symptoms should prompt a 911 call
  versus a call to the clinic. Correct misconceptions before discharge to reduce preventable
  readmissions.
</Callout>

## Complications

Hemodynamic instability, if not recognized and treated promptly, progresses to a cascade of organ-system complications that dramatically increase morbidity and mortality.

### Acute Organ Failure

**Acute kidney injury (AKI):** Renal perfusion is highly dependent on MAP, and sustained MAP < 65 mmHg — even for brief periods — rapidly produces tubular ischemia and AKI. AKI complicates up to 50% of ICU admissions and markedly prolongs length of stay. See the *Acute Kidney Injury* encyclopedia entry for comprehensive detail.

**Acute respiratory distress syndrome (ARDS):** Hemodynamic instability, particularly the endothelial injury of septic shock, triggers alveolar flooding and surfactant dysfunction, producing ARDS. Mechanical ventilation with lung-protective strategies (low tidal volume, PEEP titration) is required. Mortality remains 35–45% for severe ARDS.

**Hepatic dysfunction:** Hepatic hypoperfusion produces ischemic hepatitis ("shock liver") with rapid elevation of transaminases (AST, ALT up to 10–100 × normal), coagulopathy from reduced synthetic function, and hypoglycemia from depleted glycogen stores.

**Sepsis-associated encephalopathy (SAE) / Hypoxic-ischemic encephalopathy:** Cerebral hypoperfusion produces delirium, agitation, decreased LOC, or frank coma. The brain is highly sensitive to hypotensive episodes, particularly in elderly patients with pre-existing cerebrovascular disease.

**Gastrointestinal complications:** Reduced splanchnic blood flow causes intestinal ischemia, mucosal barrier disruption, stress ulceration, and potential bacterial translocation — further feeding the inflammatory response.

### Cardiovascular Complications

**Myocardial ischemia / infarction:** Sustained hypotension reduces coronary perfusion pressure (which occurs during diastole), precipitating type 2 myocardial infarction — demand-supply mismatch in the absence of plaque rupture. This is particularly common in patients with pre-existing coronary artery disease.

**Arrhythmias:** Both tachyarrhythmias and bradyarrhythmias are common during hemodynamic instability, driven by electrolyte disturbances (hypokalemia, hypomagnesemia, hypocalcemia), sympathetic surges, ischemia, and the pro-arrhythmic effects of vasopressor medications.

**Vasopressor-associated complications:** Prolonged vasoconstrictor use can cause peripheral ischemia, digital necrosis, and mesenteric ischemia. Extravasation of vesicant vasopressors causes severe tissue necrosis requiring immediate treatment with phentolamine.

### Multiorgan Dysfunction Syndrome (MODS)

MODS — simultaneous failure of two or more organ systems — is the endpoint of severe, prolonged hemodynamic instability. The cumulative mortality of MODS rises sharply with each additional failing organ: two-organ failure carries approximately 30–40% mortality; five-organ failure exceeds 80%. Prevention through early recognition and aggressive intervention is far more effective than treatment of established MODS.

## Special Populations

### Older Adults

Age-related physiological changes significantly alter hemodynamic presentation and response to treatment in older adults:

- **Reduced cardiovascular reserve:** Decreased β-adrenergic responsiveness, lower maximum heart rate, and reduced aortic compliance mean that the compensatory tachycardia expected in younger patients may be blunted or absent. A heart rate of 90 in an elderly patient may represent the same degree of physiological stress as 130 in a young adult.
- **Baseline hypertension:** Many older adults have chronically elevated blood pressures; a "normal" MAP of 65 mmHg may represent relative hypotension for a patient whose baseline MAP is 90–100 mmHg. Individualize hemodynamic targets.
- **Polypharmacy:** Beta-blockers, calcium channel blockers, ACE inhibitors, and diuretics — commonly prescribed in older adults — may blunt compensatory responses, mask tachycardia, and exacerbate hypotension.
- **Atypical presentation:** Altered mental status may be the sole presenting sign of hemodynamic compromise; do not attribute new-onset confusion in an elderly patient to baseline dementia without excluding physiological causes.
- **Fluid management:** Older adults are at higher risk for fluid overload due to reduced renal reserve and cardiac compliance; fluid challenges should be more cautious, with careful reassessment after each bolus.

### Pregnant Patients

Pregnancy produces dramatic physiological changes in hemodynamics that alter clinical interpretation:

- Cardiac output increases by 30–50% during pregnancy (peaking at 28–32 weeks) due to increased blood volume, heart rate, and stroke volume.
- SVR decreases during normal pregnancy due to progesterone-mediated vasodilation; blood pressure normally falls in the first and second trimesters before returning toward baseline.
- Supine hypotension syndrome occurs when the gravid uterus compresses the inferior vena cava, reducing venous return; always position pregnant patients (> 20 weeks) in left lateral decubent or with a wedge under the right hip.
- Preeclampsia and eclampsia produce a unique hemodynamic profile of hypertension, increased SVR, and reduced intravascular volume despite apparent edema — requiring careful differentiation from other shock states.
- Peripartum cardiomyopathy is a rare but serious cause of cardiogenic shock in the peripartum period.

### Pediatric Patients

Hemodynamic parameters differ significantly across pediatric age groups and must be interpreted with age-specific normal values. Key differences include:

- Children primarily compensate for reduced cardiac output by increasing heart rate (not stroke volume as in adults); tachycardia is therefore a more prominent and earlier sign of hemodynamic compromise.
- Hypotension in children is a **late and often pre-terminal sign** of shock — normal blood pressure does not exclude significant hemodynamic compromise.
- Normal blood pressure, heart rate, and urine output thresholds vary by age; use age-appropriate reference tables.
- Pediatric fluid resuscitation uses weight-based dosing (10–20 mL/kg isotonic crystalloid boluses, reassessing after each).

### Patients with Chronic Cardiovascular Disease

Patients with pre-existing heart failure, coronary artery disease, valvular disease, or pulmonary hypertension have reduced hemodynamic reserve and may decompensate at hemodynamic perturbations that would be well tolerated in otherwise healthy individuals. Therapeutic targets must account for baseline hemodynamic profiles; for example, patients with severe aortic stenosis have a fixed cardiac output and cannot compensate for afterload reduction — vasodilators in this population can cause catastrophic hypotension.

## Evidence Base

The nursing management of hemodynamic stability is grounded in a robust body of evidence from landmark clinical trials, international guidelines, and professional organization standards. Foundational references include:

- **Surviving Sepsis Campaign International Guidelines** (Evans et al., 2021) — comprehensive evidence-based recommendations for hemodynamic resuscitation in sepsis and septic shock, including fluid resuscitation targets, vasopressor selection, and MAP goals.
- **SMART Trial** (Semler et al., 2018, *NEJM*) — demonstrated that balanced crystalloids (Lactated Ringer's) reduced major adverse kidney events compared to normal saline in critically ill adults, establishing the preference for balanced crystalloids in most resuscitation scenarios.
- **VASST Trial** (Russell et al., 2008, *NEJM*) — established vasopressin as a safe adjunct to norepinephrine in septic shock, with possible benefit in less severe shock.
- **ARISE, ProCESS, ProMISe Trials** (2014–2015) — demonstrated that protocolized hemodynamic resuscitation targets (EGDT) had similar outcomes to usual care in septic shock, refining the understanding of fluid and vasopressor targets.
- **ATHOS-3 Trial** (Khanna et al., 2017, *NEJM*) — supported angiotensin II (Giapreza) as a rescue vasopressor in catecholamine-refractory vasodilatory shock.
- **AACN Practice Alert: Pulmonary Artery Pressure Monitoring** (AACN, 2018) — standards for safe pulmonary artery catheter management by critical care nurses.
- **ACC/AHA Heart Failure Guidelines** (Heidenreich et al., 2022) — evidence-based management of acute decompensated heart failure, including hemodynamic support strategies.
- **AACN Essentials** (2021) — Domain 1 (Knowledge for Nursing Practice) and Domain 2 (Person-Centered Care) establish the competency framework for BSN-prepared nurses managing complex hemodynamic states.

## NCLEX-NG Formative Check

The following questions are designed to assess clinical judgment at the level expected on the Next Generation NCLEX. Use these to identify gaps in understanding and guide further study.

---

**Question 1**

A nurse is caring for a 58-year-old male admitted with decompensated heart failure. Current assessment findings: BP 88/70 mmHg, HR 118 beats/min, RR 24 breaths/min, SpO₂ 91% on 4L NC, bilateral crackles to mid-lung fields, S3 heart sound, JVD present, 3+ pitting edema bilateral lower extremities, urine output 15 mL over the past hour. Which hemodynamic profile is most consistent with this patient's clinical presentation?

A. Low preload, high SVR, high CO  
B. High preload, high SVR, low CO  
C. Low preload, low SVR, high CO  
D. High preload, low SVR, high CO

**Correct Answer: B**

**Rationale:** Decompensated heart failure with cardiogenic shock is characterized by elevated preload (volume overload evidenced by crackles, JVD, S3, and edema), compensatory high SVR (vasoconstriction attempting to maintain MAP), and low CO (the fundamental defect — impaired contractility producing hypotension, tachycardia, oliguria, and hypoxemia). Option C describes distributive shock (e.g., sepsis — low SVR, high CO). Option A describes hypovolemia. Option D describes distributive shock in the hyperdynamic phase.

---

**Question 2 (Select All That Apply)**

A nurse is assessing a 72-year-old woman who was admitted 6 hours ago with a hip fracture. Vital signs now: BP 102/76 mmHg (was 138/82 mmHg on admission), HR 108 beats/min, RR 20 breaths/min. The patient is confused and restless. Urine output over the last 2 hours: 22 mL total. Which findings indicate hemodynamic compromise requiring immediate escalation? Select all that apply.

A. Heart rate of 108 beats/min  
B. Systolic blood pressure of 102 mmHg  
C. Urine output of 22 mL over 2 hours  
D. Respiratory rate of 20 breaths/min  
E. New confusion and restlessness  
F. Blood pressure decrease of 36 mmHg from baseline

**Correct Answers: A, B, C, E, F**

**Rationale:** Tachycardia (A), hypotension below the patient's own baseline (B and F — a drop of ≥ 40 mmHg from baseline is clinically significant even if absolute value appears borderline), oliguria of < 0.5 mL/kg/hr (C), and new altered mental status (E) are all manifestations of the "perfusion triad" failure and indicate hemodynamic compromise requiring immediate provider notification. Respiratory rate of 20 (D) is at the upper limit of normal and is less specific in isolation; it does not independently indicate hemodynamic compromise in this scenario.

---

**Question 3 (Drop-Down)**

A nurse is titrating a norepinephrine infusion for a patient with septic shock. Current infusion rate: 0.15 mcg/kg/min. MAP is 58 mmHg. The provider has ordered to maintain MAP ≥ 65 mmHg.

Complete the following nursing action by selecting the best option from each drop-down:

The nurse should [**increase**] the norepinephrine infusion rate because the MAP is [**below**] the prescribed target, and should reassess the MAP and hemodynamic parameters [**within 5–15 minutes**] of the change.

**Rationale:** Norepinephrine should be titrated upward in small increments when MAP falls below the prescribed target. A MAP of 58 mmHg is below the evidence-based minimum of 65 mmHg for adequate organ perfusion in septic shock. Reassessment within 5–15 minutes is appropriate to evaluate response and determine whether further titration is needed. Delays in titration increase the duration of inadequate organ perfusion.

**Question 4**

The nurse receives the following hemodynamic data from a pulmonary artery catheter for a patient in the ICU:

- MAP: 62 mmHg  
- CVP: 2 mmHg  
- PAWP: 4 mmHg  
- Cardiac Index: 1.8 L/min/m²  
- SVR: 1,600 dynes/sec/cm⁵

Which intervention is the PRIORITY action for this patient?

A. Administer a 500 mL IV bolus of 0.9% normal saline  
B. Begin a dobutamine infusion at 5 mcg/kg/min  
C. Begin a nitroglycerin infusion at 10 mcg/min  
D. Administer furosemide 40 mg IV push

**Correct Answer: A**

**Rationale:** The hemodynamic profile — low CVP (2 mmHg), low PAWP (4 mmHg), low cardiac index (1.8, below the threshold of 2.2 for cardiogenic shock), and elevated SVR (compensatory vasoconstriction) — is consistent with **hypovolemic shock**. The heart is receiving inadequate preload; there is insufficient volume in the ventricle to generate adequate stroke volume despite high vascular resistance. The priority intervention is fluid resuscitation to optimize preload. Dobutamine (B) increases contractility but will not correct inadequate preload and may precipitate hypotension. Nitroglycerin (C) and furosemide (D) would further reduce preload, worsening the patient's condition.

**Question 5**

A nurse is preparing to administer norepinephrine to a patient in septic shock. The patient does not yet have a central venous catheter; a 16-gauge peripheral IV catheter is in the right antecubital fossa. Which action is MOST appropriate?

A. Withhold the norepinephrine until a central line is inserted  
B. Administer the norepinephrine via the peripheral IV as a temporary measure, monitoring closely for extravasation  
C. Administer a 1 L normal saline bolus instead, and reassess in 2 hours  
D. Request a different vasopressor that is safe for peripheral administration only

**Correct Answer: B**

**Rationale:** Current evidence and clinical guidelines (including the Surviving Sepsis Campaign) support short-term peripheral administration of vasopressors — including norepinephrine — as a bridge while central access is established. Delaying vasopressor administration to wait for central line placement (A) prolongs organ hypoperfusion and is not recommended. The nurse should administer norepinephrine via the peripheral IV with close monitoring for extravasation, site integrity, and clinical response, while simultaneously facilitating urgent central line placement. Additional fluid resuscitation (C) alone is insufficient when the patient remains in refractory shock. There is no vasopressor categorically "safe for peripheral use only" (D); the peripheral route is a temporary measure for all vasopressors.
